Scan&Go Arcade – Motion‑controlled interactive game

A single‑player arcade installation where people use hand gestures to select bubbles on screen, race against the clock and compete for the top of the leaderboard at LAVS, a visual and sound art exhibition.

Client University project
Domain Interactive installation
Role Concept, development & installation
Year 2023
01 · Concept & mechanics

An arcade game about speed, precision and replayability

Scan&Go Arcade is a single‑player interactive installation built around a simple idea: select as many bubbles as you can before time runs out. Each bubble you “touch” with your hand‑controlled cursor adds points, and at the end of the round your score is saved in a leaderboard that keeps people coming back to beat each other.

The goal was to design something instantly understandable, fast to play and compelling enough that visitors at an exhibition would want to try it more than once — even if they had no previous experience with videogames.

The installation had to work like a “pick‑up‑and‑play” arcade machine: no controller, no tutorial, just step in front of the screen and intuitively understand what to do.
02 · Interaction & technology

Motion tracking as the only controller

The system uses a motion detection sensor to recognise when someone stands in front of the screen and to track their hand movement. That motion is translated into a cursor inside the game, so moving your hand in the air feels like moving a virtual pointer.

To keep the experience accessible, there are no physical buttons: actions are triggered simply by hovering the cursor over each button for a few seconds. This dwell‑time interaction makes the game inclusive for people with little or no gaming background.

The project was developed in Processing, from early concept and mechanics definition to code implementation, sensor integration and several rounds of testing and tuning to make the cursor responsive and the game flow feel fair.

What I handled in this phase
  • Definition of core game loop and difficulty curve (round length, spawn rate, scoring).
  • Implementation in Processing, including state management for menus, gameplay and leaderboard.
  • Integration of the motion sensor and mapping of physical gestures to the on‑screen cursor.
  • User tests to adjust dwell‑time thresholds and bubble behaviour so the game feels reactive, not frustrating.
03 · Exhibition

From classroom project to public installation at LAVS

After being presented to the university board, Scan&Go Arcade was selected to be exhibited at LAVS – Laboratori d’art visual i sonor (21–30 April 2023, former prison of Mataró). During the exhibition it functioned as a fully open interactive piece: people could walk up, play, see the leaderboard and try to climb to the top positions.

The installation required building not just the game, but the whole experience: physical setup, sensor placement, projection, and calibration so that different heights and distances still translated into a smooth interaction.

04 · Game UI

Menus, game states and leaderboard

The visual language is simple and bold to remain readable from a distance inside a gallery. Each screen is focused on a single action: start a game, follow the bubbles, read your score or check the leaderboard. This structure helps visitors understand where they are in the experience at all times.

What this project taught me

Interactive installations live or die on clarity

In a public space you only have a few seconds to explain the rules. The UI, pacing and feedback must make the game self‑explanatory.

Sensors require iteration

Mapping hand movement to a cursor that feels natural takes rounds of testing, calibration and small tweaks to thresholds.

Exhibition context shapes design decisions

Light conditions, distance and crowd flow influenced choices in colour, typography size and how long a round should last.

A simple mechanic can still feel competitive

Adding a persistent leaderboard turned a basic “tap the bubbles” game into something that encouraged replay and social interaction.
